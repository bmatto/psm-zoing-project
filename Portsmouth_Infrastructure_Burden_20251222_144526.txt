PORTSMOUTH INFRASTRUCTURE BURDEN & FISCAL SUSTAINABILITY ANALYSIS
================================================================================

This analysis examines the relationship between zoning density, tax revenue,
and estimated municipal service costs.

KEY CONCEPT: Lower-density zones require MORE infrastructure per household
(roads, utilities, emergency services) but generate LESS tax revenue per acre.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. INFRASTRUCTURE BURDEN:
   Single-family zones require 295,950 feet of roads/utilities
   Multi-family zones require 185,480 feet of roads/utilities
   Single-family zones need 1.1x more infrastructure per household

2. FISCAL EFFICIENCY:
   Single-family fiscal ratio: 15.9 (revenue/cost)
   Multi-family fiscal ratio: 23.9 (revenue/cost)
   Multi-family zones are 1.5x more fiscally sustainable

3. TAX BURDEN EQUITY:
   Despite higher property values, single-family homeowners in large-lot zones
   impose higher infrastructure costs per capita on the municipality.
   The sprawling nature of low-density development means:
   - More road miles to maintain per household
   - Longer utility lines per household
   - Greater distances for emergency services
   - Lower tax revenue per acre of land

4. IMPLICATIONS:
   Residents in SRA zones (1 acre minimums) may contribute less in taxes
   relative to the municipal services their large lots demand.
   Multi-family zones effectively subsidize infrastructure costs for
   lower-density single-family neighborhoods.

================================================================================
METHODOLOGY NOTES
================================================================================

Infrastructure cost estimates based on:
• Minimum lot frontage requirements (linear feet per parcel)
• $500/linear foot for combined road + utility infrastructure
• Does not include: water/sewer capacity, school buses, trash collection
• Actual costs vary based on terrain, existing infrastructure, etc.

Fiscal Sustainability Ratio = Property Tax Revenue ÷ Infrastructure Cost
• Higher ratio = more revenue per dollar of infrastructure
• <20 = concerning fiscal burden
• 20-50 = moderate sustainability
• >50 = strong fiscal contributor
